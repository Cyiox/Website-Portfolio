As an Island Nation, Puerto Rico consumes about 70 times more electricty than it can produce locally. (https://www.eia.gov/states/RQ/analysis). PR also has reserves of fosill fuels nor produces any fosil fuels locally. 

58% of PR's electricity comes from petrolium, 31% from natural gas, 10% from coal and 7% from renewables. Overall, 93% of Puerto Rico's energy comes from fossil fuels.

The commercial sector consumes about 47% of Puerto Rico's electricity,  the residential sector accounts for 43%, and the industrial sector makes up about 10%

 PREPA's heavy reliance on petroleum means that Puerto Rican power prices fluctuate with international petroleum prices and vary monthly with  fuel and purchased power costs. (Striat of hormiuiz).

The 766-megawatt Costa Sur is the largest power plant in Puerto Rico and represents 55% of the island's natural gas-fired electricity generating capacity

The regasified LNG is used to fuel electricity generation at the  766-megawatt Costa Sur power plant and the 580-megawatt EcoEléctrica  power plant.

 In 2023, LNG imports to Puerto Rico reached a new high of 82 billion  cubic feet, an increase from 50 billion cubic feet in 2022.[93](https://www.eia.gov/states/#93)

REPA is exploring the use of natural gas-fired power plants and  renewable energy with battery storage technology to provide a reliable  electricity supply.[97](https://www.eia.gov/states/#97) Puerto Rico's per capita natural gas consumption is about one-fourth of the U.S. average.[98](https://www.eia.gov/states/#98),[99](https://www.eia.gov/states/#99)

Puerto Rico's renewable resources include solar energy, wind energy, hydropower, and biomass.[100](https://www.eia.gov/states/#100) Under the Puerto Rico Energy Public Policy Act, PREPA must obtain 40%  of its electricity supply from renewable resources by 2025, 60% by 2040, and 100% by 2050.[101](https://www.eia.gov/states/#101) To meet the 2020 Integrated Resource Plan goals, PREPA would need to  procure 3,750 megawatts of renewable energy and 1,500 megawatts of  battery energy storage. However, by mid-2023, none of the original 18  approved projects had commenced operations.[102](https://www.eia.gov/states/#102) In January 2025, new Governor González Colón sent three bills to the  Legislature aimed at curtailing the interim renewable resource targets,  as well as the option to extend the use of coal-fired power until stable electricity generation is procured to meet demand without undue burden  on consumers.[103](https://www.eia.gov/states/#103)

In 2024, two renewable energy projects came online: the 26-megawatt  Punta Lima wind farm resumed operations and a utility-scale (1 megawatt  or larger) solar photovoltaic (PV) installation with 4.4 megawatts of  total generation capacity with 2-megawatts of battery energy storage.[104](https://www.eia.gov/states/#104) According to PREPA, in fiscal year 2023 (which ended June 30, 2023), 2% of total electricity generation came from renewable energy sources.[105](https://www.eia.gov/states/#105) In 2023, solar PV accounted for 42% of total renewable generation  capacity, while hydroelectric accounted for 28% and wind power accounted for 20%. The remainder of renewable generation capacity came from other resources, such as landfill gas-fueled facilities.[106](https://www.eia.gov/states/#106) Three renewable energy projects are scheduled to come online by 2026:  three solar PV projects, with a total of 122 megawatts of generating  capacity. One project would also include a 55-megawatt battery energy  storage facility.[107](https://www.eia.gov/states/#107)
